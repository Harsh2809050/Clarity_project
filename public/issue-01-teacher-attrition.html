Issue #01  ·  May 2026  ·  Education & Leadership

Your school has a ₹8 crore lobby and a ₹8 lakh teacher.
Guess which one is leaving.

with Mahesh Balakrishnan  ·  IB Strategist & Education Architect

What this issue is about

Teacher attrition is the single most expensive, most ignored, and most fixable problem in Indian schools today. Every other problem — leadership gaps, curriculum quality, student happiness, parent trust — traces back to this one.

February: A teacher quietly updates their resume.
March: A competitor calls with a 45% salary offer.
April: They hand in their resignation.
May: The school posts a job listing and starts over.

What leaves with that teacher isn't just a name on a timetable. It's seven years of relationships with students and parents. It's institutional knowledge. It's trust that took a decade to build.

The real cost of one departure

₹8 – ₹20 lakh

Recruitment fees + onboarding + IB training + year-one productivity gap
+ parent withdrawals. Nobody budgets for this. That's why it keeps happening.

3 Key Insights

1

It's not always the salary — it's everything the salary represents

When a teacher accepts a 45% raise from a competitor, the salary offer was the permission slip for a decision they'd already made. No career path. No recognition. New management that made them prove themselves again. KPIs rolled out without explanation. The money just made it easy to leave.

2

Schools that keep teachers for 7+ years do one thing differently

They build internal career ladders. Class teacher → section head → department lead → director of academics. They run cross-training programmes instead of poaching pre-certified talent. They involve teachers in decisions before announcing them. The loyalty of a teacher you grew is worth more than the credentials of one you hired away from someone else.

3

Indian teachers are now a global export — whether you like it or not

English-speaking, well-trained Indian educators are being actively recruited by international schools in Dubai, Singapore, and across Southeast Asia and Africa. A school that doesn't invest in its people is no longer competing with the school down the road. It's competing with a tax-free salary package in Abu Dhabi.

"The building doesn't teach children. People do."

— Mahesh Balakrishnan, IB Strategist & Education Architect

This week's takeaway

India doesn't have a curriculum crisis. It has a people crisis. The schools that solve it first — by treating teachers like the asset they are, not the cost they appear to be — will own the next decade.
